Ecodesign stoves replacing Fireplaces and Older Stoves

Upgrading stoves can change emissions, but it does not automatically guarantee cleaner air.

The Stove Industry Association (SIA) and its members suggest that replacing fireplaces and older stoves with modern Ecodesign models will improve air quality, yet evidence does not always support this. Some claims about reductions in pollutants like PM2.5 and PAHs are overstated. Not all stove replacements lead to meaningful emission reductions, and the promotion of Ready To Burn wood and new stoves will not, in all cases, deliver the air quality improvements the industry claims. Consumers and policymakers need to consider all relevant factors, including stove efficiency, maintenance, and usage, when assessing environmental impact.

This page shows how emissions can change under the replacement scenario shown, comparing the baseline with Ecodesign stoves using Ready To Burn certified wood.

While Ecodesign stoves meet regulatory limits for some pollutants under test conditions, this does not mean emissions are lower for every pollutant in every case. Real-world emissions from Ecodesign stoves are typically higher than laboratory test results and vary with fuel and use conditions. Emission factors for Ecodesign stoves using Ready To Burn wood can vary from year to year depending on the mix of wood fuel types used.

Use the pollutant controls to switch between Air Pollutants, Greenhouse Gases, Heavy Metals and Particulate Matter, or pick an individual pollutant, then select a year to compare the estimates. In some cases, pollutants may decrease; in others, they may stay the same or increase, such as PAHs or methane.

These results are comparative estimates, based on data from the UK's National Atmospheric Emissions Inventory (NAEI) and other published sources. The NAEI combines emission factors, fuel-use data, and modelling from surveys, industry statistics, and official energy records to produce national estimates. Emission factors are based on laboratory tests that directly measure emissions from real fireplaces and stoves burning different fuels under controlled conditions. These figures are intended to illustrate differences between appliance types and fuels. They are not predictions of emissions from individual homes.
